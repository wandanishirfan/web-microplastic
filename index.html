MICROPLASTICS

An Invisible Threat to Environmental & Human Health

🔬

WHAT ARE THEY?

Tiny plastic particles <5mm that form from larger plastics breaking down or are manufactured for products like cosmetics and textiles.

14M
TONS entering oceans annually

Learn More About Types

Primary microplastics small-sized manufactured plastic (microbeads in cosmetics, pellets for manufacturing).

Secondary microplastics result from larger plastic items breaking down through weathering, UV exposure, and physical forces.

Common types include polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polyethylene terephthalate (PET), and polystyrene (PS).

🌍

WHERE ARE THEY?

Everywhere - oceans, drinking water, food, air, and even human blood, placenta, and breast milk.

100%
of tested human samples contain microplastics

Environmental Distribution

Marine environments: From surface waters to deep-sea sediments, with particularly high concentrations in enclosed seas.

Freshwater: Lakes, rivers, and even groundwater systems show significant contamination.

Terrestrial systems: Soil contamination occurs through sewage sludge, littering, and atmospheric deposition.

Remote areas: Found in Arctic sea ice, Antarctic waters, and high mountain lakes - truly global contamination.

Detailed Exposure Pathways

Food chain: Bioaccumulation occurs as microplastics move up the food chain, with highest levels in top predators.

Drinking water: Both tap and bottled water contain microplastics, with bottled water having higher concentrations.

Air: Indoor air typically contains more microplastics than outdoor air, mainly from textiles, building materials, and household items.

Personal care: Products applied to skin may contain microbeads or be packaged in plastics that leach compounds.

MICROBIOME DISRUPTION

Linked to endocrine disruption, oxidative stress, and reproductive harm. Particles can cross blood-brain barrier and placenta.

Emerging Health Research

Endocrine disruption: Chemicals like BPA and phthalates interfere with hormone systems, potentially affecting development, reproduction, and metabolism.

Immune system: Microplastics can trigger immune responses and chronic inflammation, potentially contributing to autoimmune conditions.

Gut health: Studies show microplastics alter gut microbiome composition and may damage intestinal lining.

Cellular damage: Nano-sized particles can enter cells and disrupt normal functions, potentially causing DNA damage.

Developmental effects: Prenatal and early life exposure may have long-term health consequences that are still being studied.

Source Details & Numbers

Shellfish: Up to 10.5 microplastic particles per gram in mussels and oysters, which are filter feeders that concentrate particles.

Bottled water: Average of 325 microplastic particles per liter, compared to 5.5 particles per liter in tap water.

Synthetic clothing: A single wash of polyester clothing can release 700,000+ microfibers.

Tire wear: Produces 1kg of microplastic particles per car annually, contributing ~28% of ocean microplastics.

Tea bags: A single plastic tea bag can release billions of micro/nano plastic particles when steeped.

Advanced Solutions & Research

Improved wastewater treatment: Advanced filtration technologies can remove up to 99% of microplastics from wastewater.

Biodegradable alternatives: Research into truly biodegradable plastics from renewable resources is advancing.

Microplastic capture devices: Technologies like fiber-catching laundry balls and improved vacuum filters can reduce household microplastic emissions.

Circular economy approaches: Redesigning products for reuse, recycling, and reduced plastic dependency.

Bioremediation: Some microorganisms and enzymes show promise in breaking down certain types of microplastics.
